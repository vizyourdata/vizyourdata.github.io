Blocks,Mined Over Time

Every ten minutes, somewhere on Earth, a computer wins a lottery and writes the next page of a ledger nobody owns. Do that 950,000 times and you get Bitcoin. Here's what's actually happening — in plain words.

Pick a block up top. Watch the spark run the chain down to it.

01 The Ledger

A block is a page in a notebook everyone keeps a copy of.

A blockchain is a public ledger — a notebook that thousands of computers worldwide all hold an identical copy of. Nobody owns the master; they all agree on it.

You don't write one line at a time. Transactions get bundled into a block — a page — and a fresh page is added about every ten minutes. Each block is cryptographically linked to the one before it, so altering an old page shatters every page after it. That's what makes the history tamper-evident — and that's the chain.

Block height only ever goes up · ~950,000 pages and counting · one every ~10 min

02 Proof of Work

Mining is a race to earn the right to write the next page.

Who gets to add the next block, and why trust them? Thousands of computers race to solve a deliberately useless puzzle — guess-and-check, trillions of times a second. First to crack it wins the right to write the block and pockets newly minted bitcoin plus fees.

The puzzle is brutal to solve but instant to check, so everyone can verify the winner really did the work. The wasted electricity is the security: to fake history you'd have to out-compute the entire honest network at once.

Total guessing power = hashrate · measured in quintillions of hashes per second

03 The Heartbeat

Difficulty keeps the ten-minute pulse, no matter who joins.

More miners would crack the puzzle faster — so every two weeks the network re-tunes how hard it is. Power floods in, difficulty rises; miners quit, it eases. The goal is a steady ten-minute heartbeat that holds whether the network is tiny or planet-scale.

It's the chain breathing at a constant rate by design — a thermostat made of math.

Re-adjusts every 2,016 blocks (~2 weeks) · target spacing 10 min

04 Hard Money

The reward halves every four years — forever, until 21 million.

The prize for mining a block isn't fixed. Every ~four years it halves: 50 → 25 → 12.5 → 6.25 → 3.125 BTC. That schedule is pure arithmetic, visible decades in advance, and it ends at a hard cap of 21 million coins, ever.

Each "halving" is a built-in supply squeeze — the single most-watched recurring event on the whole timeline.

Halves every 210,000 blocks · last new coin mined around the year 2140

05 The Business

Mining is an industry: electricity in, coins out.

Behind the math is a market. Miners spend real money on rigs and power and earn coins — so they're a constant sell-pressure and a deeply price-sensitive business. Price climbs, more miners pile in, difficulty rises to match. A halving cuts their revenue overnight, and the weakest rigs go dark.

Price, hashrate and difficulty chase each other in a loop you can watch unfold over the years.

When price > mining cost, miners grow · when it flips, they capitulate

06 The Waiting Room

Fees are Bitcoin's surge pricing for block space.

A block holds only so much. When more people want in than will fit, they bid up the fee (priced in sats per byte) to jump the queue into the next block. Everyone who's waiting sits in the mempool — the chain's waiting room.

So fees are a live demand gauge: calm when the chain is quiet, screaming during a frenzy. This is Bitcoin's answer to what Ethereum calls "gas."

More demand → fuller blocks → higher sat/vByte → longer mempool queue

07 What It's For

Underneath every use, it's just transactions filling blocks.

People treat Bitcoin as digital gold — a scarce, hard-to-seize store of value — and as money: plain payments on-chain, or near-instant cheap ones over a second layer called Lightning. Newer experiments even stuff art and data straight into blocks.

Unlike Ethereum, Bitcoin has no rich "smart contracts" — its scripting is deliberately limited. But whatever the use, it lands the same place: more transactions, fuller blocks, back to the ten-minute heartbeat where we started.
