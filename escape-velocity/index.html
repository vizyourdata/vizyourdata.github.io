T-0 Ignition

A rocket company opened on the Nasdaq.

On the night of 11 June 2026, SpaceX priced its shares at $135 apiece. Sell 555.6 million of them and you raise about $75 billion — one of the largest US listings ever — at a valuation near $1.75 trillion.

The next morning the ticker SPCX opened, and buyers didn't wait. First trades cleared near $161 — a 19% pop in the opening minutes — and a private company twenty-four years in the making belonged to anyone with a brokerage app.

Priced $135 · first trades ~$161 · raise ~$75B · SPCX on Nasdaq

01 Liftoff

It took twenty-four years to reach this altitude.

SpaceX was founded in 2002 and nearly died in 2008, after its first three Falcon 1 rockets failed. The fourth reached orbit, a NASA contract followed, and the valuation has bent upward ever since.

$12B in 2015. $100B by 2021. $350B by the end of 2024. On the chart the line looks almost gentle — but the axis is logarithmic: each gridline is ten times the one below it. A near-straight climb here is exponential growth, and the IPO is the final stage lighting.

2015 $12B → 2021 $100B → 2024 $350B → 2026 IPO $1.75T · a ~145× climb in eleven years

02 Stage I

The trick was paying everyone in stock.

For years SpaceX ran lean on cash and generous on equity. Engineers, technicians, welders, even cafeteria staff were granted stock options instead of top-of-market salaries — a bet on a company whose shares you couldn't actually sell.

While SpaceX stayed private, that paper was illiquid: real on a spreadsheet, unspendable in the world. An IPO is the moment paper becomes cash. Twenty-four years of deferred pay came due in a single morning.

“It's life-changing for thousands of people who took a chance on a rocket company that, for a long time, paid in promises.”

03 Staging

Four thousand four hundred millionaires.

An analysis of the cap table put the number of employees and ex-employees whose holdings cross $1 million at more than 4,400 — about one in five of the workforce. Roughly 400 of them clear $100 million.

These aren't only executives. Reporting after the debut surfaced a former welder, a 2011 intern who'd defied his parents to take the job, line cooks from the company cafeteria — all suddenly seven figures. Each dot below is a hundred people; the lit ones woke up rich.

4,400+ over $1M · ~400 over $100M · 1 in 5 of ~22,000 staff

04 Orbit

Now the rocket company is worth more than the cars.

At $1.75 trillion, SpaceX debuted larger than Tesla — the other Musk company, and for years the more valuable one. It slots in around the seventh-largest US public company on day one, a rung that took Apple and Nvidia decades to climb.

A business built on reusable rockets and a satellite-internet constellation is now, by market value, among the most important companies in America — priced in a single morning by the same crowd that woke 4,400 of its own people into a new tax bracket.
