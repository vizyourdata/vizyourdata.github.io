First · The Table

Every meal is set
on the same ocean

For the 13 million people of the Pacific islands, the ocean is not scenery. It is the table itself: it feeds the reef fish, waters the clouds, steadies the gardens. Scientists have been taking its temperature around these islands since 1850.

Sea-surface temperature anomaly · Pacific island average · 1850–2025

176 candles on the table. Each stripe is one year of sea-surface temperature around the islands, woven like a mat from 1850 to 2025.

Now add the heat. Cool years in lagoon blue, warm years in fire. For a century the weave barely shifts — a cool stripe here, a warm El Niño there.

The ten hottest years in this entire record are the last ten. 2016 through 2025 — every single one. Nothing else in 176 years comes close.

And it is everyone's table. In 20 of 21 territories, the hottest sea ever measured has come since 2015. Pick an island; the weave ends in fire almost everywhere.

Second · The Rain

The sky sets
the drinks menu

On a high volcanic island, rain fills rivers. On an atoll — Tuvalu, Nauru, Tokelau — there are no rivers. Rain on the roof is the water supply. And a warming Pacific doesn't just bring more rain or less; it brings whiplash.

Annual rainfall anomaly · Tuvalu · 1979–2025 · % vs 1991–2020 normal

Tuvalu, year by year. Teal bars are wet years, brown bars are dry. The dots underneath mark El Niño — the ocean pattern that steals the rain.

2011: the rain simply didn't come. Tuvalu's driest year in this record — 42% below normal. The government declared a state of emergency; fresh water was shipped and flown in.

TUVALU 2011 · −42% RAINFALL · STATE OF EMERGENCY DECLARED SEPT 2011

Nauru lives on a swing. From 54% below normal in 1999 to 67% above in 2019. When your reservoir is the sky, this is what supply risk looks like.

Drought is a meal skipped. Less rain means thirsty taro pits, brackish wells, empty tanks. Explore your island's rain ledger above.

Third · The Garden

What grows when
the weather wobbles

Taro, banana, coconut: the holy trinity of the Pacific plate. The UN has tracked island harvests since 1961. The garden's lesson is not a smooth decline — it is fragility: how fast a staple can fall when a shock arrives.

Taro yield · Samoa · 1961–2024 · kg per hectare

Samoa's taro, the national dish. For thirty years the yield line holds its ground — six, seven tonnes a hectare, season after season.

July 1993: taro leaf blight lands in Samoa. Within a year, yields fall to 1,500 kg/ha — about a quarter of late-80s levels. Exports collapsed. A whole cuisine changed in eighteen months.

ONE PATHOGEN · ONE CROP · −77% · THIS IS WHAT FRAGILITY MEANS
WARMER, WETTER EXTREMES WIDEN THE DOOR FOR PESTS & BLIGHT

Across the region, the trinity wobbles. Since the 1960s: banana yields down 79% in French Polynesia, 56% in Tonga. Coconut down 41% in Solomon Islands. Not everywhere — Fiji's plantations tripled — but the declines cluster where gardens are smallholder and exposed.

Interlude · The Bill

When the sky
writes the bill

Does the weather really reach the plate? Honest answer: not every year, and not everywhere — at national scale the dice are loaded, not fixed. But when a drought lands on a rain-fed harvest, the ledger is unambiguous. Watch Fiji.

Fiji · sea temperature, rainfall & harvest on one page · 1979–2024

Three rows, one country. The warming sea along the top, the rain in the middle, the harvest underneath. In 1987 and 1998, El Niño took Fiji's rain — −22% and −24% — and the harvest followed it down, −21% and −20%.

FIJI'S HEADLINE CROP INDEX IS DOMINATED BY RAIN-FED SUGARCANE —
WHEN THE RAIN FAILS, THE CANE FAILS

1999: the rains return — the harvest jumps 56%. One wet season buys back what two dry ones took. That is what it means to eat from a rain-fed garden: the sky sets the portion.

Fourth · The Glass

Surrounded by water,
short of a drink

The cruellest line on the menu. "Safely managed" drinking water — clean, on premises, available when needed — is the UN's gold standard. In much of the Pacific, the glass is nearly empty.

Population using safely managed drinking water · 2024 · WHO/UNICEF JMP

Fifteen glasses, one for each country with data. The fill is the share of people with safely managed water. Tuvalu: 9%. Kiribati: 15%. Nauru & Vanuatu: 19%.

SDG 6.1.1 · "SAFELY MANAGED" = IMPROVED SOURCE, ON PREMISES,
AVAILABLE WHEN NEEDED, FREE OF CONTAMINATION

A quarter-century, barely a ripple. Since 2000, Tuvalu has gone from 8% to 9%. Kiribati from 10% to 15%. The lines are flat while the droughts sharpen.

And the gap runs urban to rural. In Samoa, 92% of town dwellers have safe water — 56% in the villages. In Palau, 98 vs 57. The further you live from the pipe, the closer you live to the drought.

Six glasses we cannot even fill in. Papua New Guinea, Solomon Islands, FSM, the Marshalls, Cook Islands, Tokelau — no national estimate exists at all. The region's most populous country is a blank.

NO SDG 6.1.1 "SAFELY MANAGED" ESTIMATE · JMP 2025
YOU CANNOT FIX WHAT YOU CANNOT MEASURE

Fifth · The Kitchen

Where the work
gets done

The Pacific is not waiting politely for the bill. Since 1950, island governments have signed or joined over a thousand fisheries-management instruments — treaties, agreements, access arrangements — to keep protein on the plate as the ocean shifts.

Fisheries-management instruments in force · all 22 Pacific territories · cumulative

Adaptation looks like this: rainwater tanks and desalination on the atolls, blight-resistant taro bred back into Samoa's gardens, tuna treaties that move with the fish. The data says the heat is at the table. It also says the kitchen is busy.

Why this story

Climate stories about the Pacific are usually told in metres of sea-level rise. But long before the water reaches the doorstep, it reaches the dinner table — as a hotter sea, a missed rainy season, a blighted garden, an empty tank.

This story puts the region's own numbers — SPC's climate indicators, FAO's harvest records, the WHO/UNICEF water ledger — on one plate, so the scale of the change can be read in the most human unit there is: a meal.

Data

  • Sea-surface temperature, rainfall anomalies & fisheries instruments: SPC Pacific Data Hub, Climate Change dataflow (DF_CLIMATE_CHANGE 1.0). SST is EEZ-averaged vs a 1993–2012 baseline; rainfall vs 1991–2020.
  • Crop yields: PDH.stat Agricultural Production dataflow (DF_AGRICULTURAL_PRODUCTION 1.0), original source FAOSTAT. Small-island values are partly FAO estimates.
  • Drinking water: SDG 6.1.1 "safely managed", WHO/UNICEF Joint Monitoring Programme 2025, via PDH.stat (DF_SDG 4.3).

Method & honesty

  • All data is annual and national — the finest resolution PDH.stat provides. No causal claims are made between single weather years and single harvests; documented events (the 2011 Tuvalu drought, the 1993 taro leaf blight) carry the narrative.
  • Made for the Pacific Dataviz Challenge 2026. Data accessed June 2026.
