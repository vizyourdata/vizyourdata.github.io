The Hebrew Bible keeps a thread it never lets go of — a single family line, traced name by name from the first man to the shepherd-king. Drawn as one river it floods, narrows to a lone survivor, and breaks at last into twelve. From Adam to David, the whole genealogy in one figure.

Scripture's genealogies are the chapters everyone skims — the begats, page on page of names. But laid end to end they make a single, stubborn claim: that one unbroken line runs from creation all the way to the throne. Scroll, and watch the river fill.

Ten generations, one line. From Adam, the text follows a single chosen son each generation — Seth, Enosh, Kenan, on down to Noah — while the rest of mankind spreads, unnumbered, beneath.

Then the Flood narrows everything to one house. Every other line ends at the water. Humanity passes through Noah alone — the river's great pinch, a whole world funnelled through a single man.

Three sons become the nations. Japheth and Ham fan out into the seventy peoples of the earth — the Table of Nations — while Shem quietly carries the thread on.

Why this story

The genealogies are the parts everyone skips. But they are doing something audacious: insisting that history has a single spine — that you can lay a finger on Adam and trace, name by name, all the way to a king.

As a shape, it is a river that should have died a dozen times: drowned in the Flood, stalled in barren wives, handed to younger sons with no claim, married into by outsiders — a Moabite, a woman at a well. It never does. Drawn once, the begats stop being a list and become an argument.

Data & text

  • The line of Seth and the recorded ages: Genesis 5 (Adam → Noah).
  • The Flood and the survivors: Genesis 7–9.
  • The Table of Nations: Genesis 10 — Japheth, Ham and Shem's seventy peoples.
  • Shem to Abraham: Genesis 11.
  • The sons of Abraham, Isaac and Esau / Edom: Genesis 25 & 36.
  • The twelve sons of Jacob: Genesis 29–30 & 35.
  • Judah to David: Ruth 4:18–22 and 1 Chronicles 2. Text: King James / Masoretic.

Method & honesty

  • Band thickness is schematic: it shows how many distinct lines the chart is following at a point — not population, not number of descendants. The chosen line is drawn as a single thread; a branch is capped where the text stops naming it.
  • The seventy nations of Genesis 10, Ishmael's twelve princes and Esau's dukes are shown as bundles, not every name. The twelve tribes are ordered for legibility, with Judah set on the spine — not in birth order. "The rest of mankind" before the Flood is illustrative.
  • Ages are as the text records them (Masoretic numbers; the Septuagint and Samaritan texts differ). This charts a written genealogy, not a verified historical pedigree.
