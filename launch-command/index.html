ACT I

The cadence

Count every launch, year by year, and you get a heartbeat. For a long time it was steady. Then it flatlined. Then, in the last ten years, it went vertical.

1957 — 1990

A Cold War metronome.

Through the space race and the decades after, the world settled into a rhythm of roughly 100 to 130 launches a year. Two superpowers, launching relentlessly, kept the beat steady for a generation.

1965–1985 · ~120 launches / yr
peak of the bipolar era
1991 — 1999

Then the beat broke.

When the Soviet Union dissolved, half the world's launch program lost its funding overnight. The cadence fell by a third and kept sliding through the decade.

1985: 126 → 1999: 78
the post-Soviet drawdown
2000 — 2012

The quiet decade.

For twelve years the world launched at its slowest sustained pace since the early 1960s, averaging just ~66 a year. Spaceflight had become routine, expensive, and rare.

2000s average · ~66 launches / yr
the lowest sustained cadence
2013 — 2026

Then it went vertical.

Reusable rockets and mega-constellations rewrote the economics. In 2025 the world launched 330 times, nearly triple the Cold War peak, and the curve is still climbing.

2021: 146 → 2025: 330
the New Space surge
2019 — 2026

One company, one constellation.

A single satellite network explains much of the spike. Starlink alone went from 2 launches in 2019 to 122 in 2025, more than a third of every launch on Earth that year.

The lead was built long ago

Russia holds the record. It barely flies anymore.

The Soviet Union and Russia account for 3,384 launches, the most of any program in history. But look at the last five years and the picture inverts: the United States and China now own the sky, while Russia has fallen behind even the mid-tier powers.

The share of the world's launches tells the story bluntly. What was a two-thirds Soviet monopoly in the Cold War is now a US and China contest, with Russia down to a rounding error.

ACT III

The doorways

Forty-five spaceports are scattered across the planet. Almost everything leaves through four of them.

A handful of gates

Four sites. Three-quarters of everything.

Plesetsk and Baykonur, both Soviet-built, are still the two busiest launch sites on Earth, together with Cape Canaveral and Vandenberg in the United States. These four spaceports account for 75.5% of all 7,335 launches ever flown.

The takeaway

The monuments are Soviet.
The momentum is new.

The Cold War built the record books and the two busiest launch pads on the planet. But the present belongs to a reusable-rocket surge out of America and a fast-rising China, lifting more mass, more often, than any era before it.
