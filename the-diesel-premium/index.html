Two Pumps, One Price

From 2003 through 2021, 969 weeks, the two fuels tracked each other so closely the gap averaged just 4.5p a litre. Diesel cost a little more. Drivers grumbled. But the two prices were really one price.

2003 → 2021 · avg gap 4.49p · 969 weeks

The Exception

It even flipped. In the cheap-oil slump of 2015-16, diesel spent 13 scattered weeks cheaper than petrol, bottoming out 2.5p under. Proof the premium was economics, not a law of nature.

Diesel below petrol · Aug 2015 → Feb 2016 · min −2.53p

The First Shock

Russia's invasion of Ukraine sent both pumps to all-time records: petrol 191.5p, diesel 199.2p in July 2022. The gap stretched to 24.5p that November. Then, slowly, it healed.

4 Jul 2022 records · gap 24.45p on 14 Nov 2022

The Divergence

On 28 February 2026 war reached Iran, and only one pump really felt it. Petrol climbed 26p. Diesel jumped +50.7p in eight weeks, to within 7p of its all-time record. The gap blew past every number in the book.

16 Feb → 13 Apr 2026 · petrol +26.5p · diesel +50.7p

The Premium, Isolated

Strip the prices away and look at the gap alone: a flat 4.5p hum for two decades, a 24p spike in 2022, then a 34p wall in 2026. The premium is no longer noise on the price of oil. It is the story.

Record gap 33.97p · 13 Apr 2026 · prior record 24.45p
01
01It isn't the crude

Both pumps drink from the same barrel.
Only one of them is short.

Petrol and diesel start as the same oil: what differs is the refining. Britain makes more petrol than it needs and exports the surplus. Diesel it cannot make enough of: self-sufficient as recently as 2011, the UK now imports 2.5 times what it refines. Petrol's price is set by a surplus at home. Diesel's is set by what a cargo costs at sea.

Petrol: surplus, exported Diesel: 2.5× imported vs refined Self-sufficient until 2011
02Then the refineries fell

Six refineries became four.

In 2025 Britain lost two of its six remaining oil refineries: Petroineos Grangemouth, Scotland's only one, and Prax Lindsey in Lincolnshire. Every barrel they no longer crack is a barrel that now arrives by tanker. The import dependence deepened in the worst possible year to deepen it.

6 → 4 UK refineries 2 closures in 2025 Grangemouth · Scotland's last
03Then the strait

When war reached the Gulf, only one pump flinched.

The strikes on Iran put the Strait of Hormuz, the channel for the Gulf diesel and jet fuel Britain now depends on, at risk. In the first three weeks of March 2026, petrol rose 12p a litre. Diesel rose 25p. Same war, same forecourts, double the pain: only one of the two fuels has to run the strait.

The takeaway

Petrol's price is made in Britain.
Diesel's is made at sea.

A record 34p premium wasn't set by the price of oil. It was set by what Britain can no longer refine for itself, and by every mile of water its diesel now has to cross.
