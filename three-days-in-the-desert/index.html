Three Days in the Desert

The festival came back from the pandemic bigger than ever. Then, year by year, the lineup began to disappear. This is where it went.

The Return

When Coachella reopened in 2022 after two cancelled years, it came back at full volume – 179 acts across the weekend, the largest bill in its history.

The Slide

That was the peak. Every edition since has been smaller – 167, 153, 151 – and just 139 in 2026. A −22% cut in four years, the leanest lineup in a decade.

The Quiet Day

The loss isn't shared evenly. Sunday has been hollowed out – down −25% since 2018 – fading from a co-headline night into the weekend's thinnest day.

The New Center

As Saturday – long the anchor – collapsed, Friday held firm and seized the crown, topping the bill two years running. The weekend's center of gravity slid to opening night.

It wasn't the space

They didn't shrink the desert.
They added to it.

The obvious suspects – less land, fewer stages – are wrong. Coachella expanded after the pandemic: the Quasar DJ stage arrived in 2024, and Do LaB grew from 45 to 60 artists. The grounds got bigger. The bill got smaller.

+1 new stage · Quasar, 2024 Do LaB 45 → 60 artists 3 DJ stages now

It was the math

A few stars now cost what a hundred acts used to.

Touring and production costs jumped 30–40% since 2020. Headline fees run into the millions – Justin Bieber reportedly took $10M+ for a single set. With a fixed budget, Coachella pours money into the top of the poster and trims the undercard. Tickets climbed too.

And it wasn't just here

The whole festival economy is contracting.

More than 100 festivals were cancelled in 2025. Roughly two-thirds of large festivals lost money in 2024. And the biggest acts increasingly skip the circuit altogether, chasing better margins on their own stadium tours. Coachella still sold out 2026 in under a week – but it's bending to the same pressure: protect the headliners, shrink the rest.

The takeaway

Coachella didn't run out of room.
It ran into the math.

A 22% smaller lineup is the visible cost of an industry that now pays a handful of stars far more – and everyone else far less.
